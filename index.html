Welcome to Tuvalu

A hidden gem in the Pacific, Tuvalu is as far as can be from other commercialized tourist resorts. Home to a festive and vibrant people, the islands of Tuvalu are teeming with flora and fauna that have become exceedingly rare throughout the world. These small strips of land in the heart of the glorious Pacific Ocean await exploration.

All you need to know

From $500

CLIMATE

Tuvalu’s landmass is not a lot, and it is surrounded on all sides by the Pacific Ocean. Therefore, ocean winds dominate the country, and you can expect little to no temperature variation because of them. Whether it's August or December, Tuvalu’s temperature rarely sways from a cool 30 degrees Celsius. As for rain, it can vary a lot from year to year, but it is mostly more than 3000 mm.

From $800

CURRENCY

Tuvalu uses Australian Currency. The country does not have a credit card system - which means you will have to rely on cheques and cash. The National Bank of Tuvalu also has limited operation hours, and it is only open 10 AM to 2 PM Monday to Thursday, and 9 AM to 1 PM on Fridays. Overseas payments exceeding $2000 are also a hassle since they require exchange approval, so make sure you plan your trip accordingly

From $600

HISTORY

The people of Tuvalu lived under tribal law and chiefs until the European trade incursions changed most of the world, and Tuvalu along with it. Christianity and Protestantism have had a deep impact on the island nation, and most of them are protestant Christians as a result of it. Tuvalu recently joined the United Nations and has been voted the most peaceful among the underdeveloped countries of the world.

Things To Do In Tuvalu

Bike Across The Island

Who doesn’t want to feel like Tom Cruise riding his bike to the airstrip in Top Gun? Since Tuvalu’s main island, Fongafale is technically just a long, thin strip, you can rent a bike from your guesthouse and ride across the 10 KM long island, enjoying a glorious view of the sea from all angles. Even if you can’t drive a bike yourself, Tuvalu’s people are courteous enough to offer to drive it for you (if you ask nicely, that is).

Funafuti Conservation Area

The Kaupule o Funafuti (The elders of Funafuti) and the government came together in 1999 to make the Funafuti Conservation Area to protect endangered species. The area is home to many species of fish, corals, algae, and invertebrates. Two of the most popular denizens of these colonies are the green sea turtle and the black noddy. You can visit the area to admire them and many other animals you would scarcely see in the wild.

Mangrove Planting

The Tuvalu National Council of Women (TNCW) and The Tuvalu Association of Non-Governmental Organizations (TANGO) have joined together for a very important task; saving the mangroves.

The mangroves are essential to Tuvalu's safety and economy. They enhance local fisheries, provide material for handicrafts and firewood, and protect the islands from tidal and storm surges.

You can play your part and make a difference in Tuvalu's fight for survival by helping the locals plant mangroves, or by donating money to help them get more efficient. We often hear of how global warming is destroying our planet, but rarely do we get the chance to do something about it firsthand, making mangrove planting in Tuvalu a very enriching and fulfilling experience

Diving and Snorkeling

If you were already enticed by the idea of visiting the Funafuti Conservation Area, this will get you even more excited; you can engage in activities like diving and snorkeling and get yourself truly immersed in the flora and fauna of Tuvalu. Experiencing the richness of life under the surface of the sea is unforgettable, but if diving is too much for you, you can always enjoy a quiet picnic and appreciate the beauty of nature from a distance.

Historical Landmarks

During World War II, these idyllic islands played a crucial role. American troops were stationed here, and airforce bases were strategically located to support allied forces. On the northeastern side of Nanumea, you can still see the old runway and the remains of World War II planes nestled in the scrub. Nearby, a landing craft wreck lies on the reef, a poignant reminder of the past.
In Nukufetau, the islet of Motulalo features another WWII airstrip, surrounded by plane wrecks that echo tales of yesteryear. Funafuti Atoll served as the main base, and WWII debris can be found along the main island of Fongafale. History buffs will love exploring the well-preserved underground bunker on the islet of Tepuka.
Funafuti is not just about wartime history; it’s also where scientists made groundbreaking discoveries. Visit the site where drilling operations proved Charles Darwin’s theory on atoll formation correct. Known as “David’s Drill,” this historic site showcases boreholes from the late 1800s that reached depths of up to 928 feet.

Tuvalu's sports culture

The Tuvalu airstrip is only used on Tuesdays and Thursdays; the rest of the time, it is the place where the locals come together to walk, talk, and most importantly, play sports! Although the locals enjoy a variety of sports like football and volleyball, the one that's really close to their hearts is their national game called Te Ano (The Ball). Te Ano is basically volleyball, but with a much heavier ball and a funny twist; losers must sing and dance for the winning team. The locals are more than happy to have tourists enjoy these games with them, both as players and as spectators.

The Merchants of Tuvalu

The backbone of Tuvalu's economy is the Tuvalu Marine Training Institute, which trains 120 cadets a year who go on to become seamen, cooks, and marine engineers. Apart from these merchant seamen, Tuvalu also boasts a rich local patisserie run by women who are experts at baking delicious rolls, cakes, and pies amongst other things.

Women's Handicraft Center

Apart from baking, the women of Tuvalu are very actively involved in making small handcrafted objects like fans, baskets, necklaces, and wood carvings. Their designs and styles represent the rich cultural roots of Tuvalu. You can visit these stalls alongside the airstrip and purchase these products to support the local industry.

Culture and Nightclubs

You probably weren't expecting it, but yes, Tuvalu has two nightclubs: The Matagigali Bar and the Tefota. The nightclubs only operate on Saturdays, where the locals enjoy their own distinctive music called Fatele (dancing music). The locals of Tuvalu are very spirited people, and dance music can be found even on random evenings and special events at the local town centers. If you have a festive spirit, the islanders will not disappoint you.

Yachting

It would be a sin to visit the beautiful islands of Tuvalu and not explore them the way the place deserves to be explored; Yachting. You can rent a Yacht through various local services and go island hopping across the nine islands of Tuvalu, taking in the beauty of the natural vistas around you in style.
